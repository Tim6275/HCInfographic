-Survival-
Emergency Service Volunteers in the South Central PA Mountains Region


Huntingdon County Emergency Management

Study Findings

Abstract

The number of volunteers in the emergency services of the south central mountains region of Pennsylvania continues to decline. As the number of recruits is reportedly diminishing (as noted in a longitudinal research series and field interviews) and the available pool of trained volunteers ages rapidly, emergency service organizational leadership is faced with the need to examine how it motivates citizens to join their organizations as well as how to retain them.

This thesis examines survey data of regional emergency service volunteers and has determined a whole of community effort (fully-integrated among the Local Community, County, State and Nation) is urgently needed to help organizational leadership gain capacity to overcome the challenges currently inhibiting effective recruitment and retention of emergency service volunteers. Additionally, a conclusion is reached regarding a perceived “volunteer famine” suspected to be occurring in the south central mountains region of Pennsylvania emergency services.

Introduction





Introduction

In rural Central Pennsylvania, community responses to emergency conditions (fires, floods, medical alarms, etc.) are predominantly accomplished by volunteer emergency service organizations. These organizations are comprised of individuals of nearly every age and demographic background who generally share a common desire to help others through community service.

In communities without volunteer services, paid emergency services exist, driven by legal requirements for communities to ensure the provision of public safety services. These services are predominantly tax-funded, and are staffed with full-time employees.

Regardless of being paid or unpaid, all must meet training and service requirements which still impacts their lifestyle. Studying the volunteer (who they are, why they volunteer) and understanding what roadblocks to success they face is vital to the future of retaining and propagating future volunteers.

Conclusion

There is evidence to suggest that there is a shortage of fresh, non-veteran volunteers coming in to replace aged and experienced volunteers in the emergency services.That shortage, which has been developing as a trend since the initial 2008 study, indicates that this will evolve into a crisis over the next 5 years if left unabated.

Immediate investment on the part of all levels of government in recruiting and sustaining the volunteer services is necessary to ensure their survival.
